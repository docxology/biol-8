## Overview

This lab introduces the practical measurement of the **cardiopulmonary system**, which includes your heart, blood vessels, and lungs working together to deliver oxygen to tissues and remove carbon dioxide. You will use foundational clinical tools—a **stethoscope**, a **blood pressure cuff (sphygmomanometer)**, a **pulse oximeter**, and a **spirometer**—to monitor cardiovascular and respiratory function at rest and after mild activity (or positional changes).

These techniques form the basis of vital signs monitoring in clinical and allied health settings. The lab emphasizes safe, hands-on, interactive tracking of your own physiological metrics.

---

## Background: The Cardiopulmonary System

The cardiopulmonary system connects the **cardiovascular system** (heart and blood vessels) with the **respiratory system** (lungs and airways).

- **The Heart:** Acts as a continuous dual pump. The right side pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs (pulmonary circulation), and the left side pumps freshly oxygenated blood to the rest of the body (systemic circulation).
- **The Lungs:** Facilitate gas exchange across millions of tiny alveolar sacs. Oxygen from inhaled air diffuses into the blood, while carbon dioxide (a metabolic waste product) diffuses from the blood into the lungs to be exhaled.
- **Vascular Network:** Arteries carry high-pressure blood away from the heart, while veins return it under lower pressure. Measuring this pressure is critical to understanding overall cardiovascular health.

During periods of increased metabolic demand (like exercise), both systems must upregulate their function. Heart rate and breathing volume increase to deliver more oxygen to working tissues and to clear out excess carbon dioxide rapidly.

## Safety and Hygiene

- **Equipment Cleaning:** Wipe down the ear pieces of the stethoscope and the finger sensor of the pulse oximeter with **70% alcohol wipes** before and after each use.
- **Spiropet Safety:** Ensure you use a **clean, disposable cardboard mouthpiece** for the spirometer. Never share mouthpieces, and never inhale through the device.
- **Physical Exertion:** Mild activity (such as doing 30 seconds of jumping jacks, walking briskly, or doing squats at your bench) is completely **optional**. Do not participate in the active portion if you have underlying medical conditions that contraindicate exertion. You may substitute resting/standing changes instead.
- **Cuff Pressure:** Do not leave the blood pressure cuff inflated on an arm for more than 1 minute. Release pressure if the subject experiences significant discomfort, tingling, or numbness.

## Part A — Heart Sounds (Stethoscopy)

The familiar sounds of the heart (often described as "lub-dup") are caused by the closing of heart valves. The first sound ("lub", S1) occurs when the atrioventricular (AV) valves close during ventricular contraction. The second sound ("dup", S2) occurs when the semilunar valves close during ventricular relaxation.

1. Clean the stethoscope earpieces with an alcohol wipe. Insert them into your ears angled slightly forward.
2. Place the diaphragm (the flat side of the chest piece) directly against the skin or a thin layer of clothing just to the left of your sternum (breastbone), around the 4th/5th intercostal space.
3. Sit quietly and listen until you easily distinguish the rhythmic "lub-dup."

## Part B — Heart Rate & Oxygen Saturation (Pulse Oximetry)

A pulse oximeter uses dual light wavelengths (red and infrared) to calculate the percentage of oxygenated hemoglobin in arterial blood. It also tracks the pulsatile flow to determine your heart rate in beats per minute (bpm). A healthy resting SpO2 is generally 95%–100%.

1. **Resting Baseline:** Clean the sensor, clip the oximeter onto your index or middle finger, and turn it on. Wait 15–30 seconds for the reading to stabilize. Record your data.
2. **Activity / Setup:** Engage in 30–60 seconds of mild physical activity (e.g., jumping jacks, deep knee bends, or walking briskly in the hallway). If you cannot exert yourself, take your measures after standing up quickly from a prolonged seated position.
3. **Post-Activity:** Immediately place your finger back in the pulse oximeter and record the numbers.

## Part C — Blood Pressure (Sphygmomanometer)

Blood pressure is expressed as a ratio of two numbers: **Systolic Pressure** (top number, pressure during ventricular contraction) over **Diastolic Pressure** (bottom number, pressure during ventricular relaxation). Normal resting BP is roughly 120/80 mm Hg.

**Protocol (Partner Activity):**
1. Have your partner sit with their arm resting on the table at roughly heart level.
2. Secure the deflated cuff securely around their bare upper arm (about 1 inch above the elbow crease). Position the dial so it is visible.
3. Place the stethoscope diaphragm over the brachial artery (just below the cuff, on the medial side of the upper arm crease).
4. Close the air valve on the bulb fully. Pump the bulb to inflate the cuff until the dial reads approximately **160 mm Hg**.
5. **Slowly** twist the valve to open it slightly, dropping the pressure by about 2-3 mm Hg per second.
6. **Listen carefully:** The pressure point on the dial where you hear the *first tapping sound* is the **Systolic pressure**. As pressure continues to drop, the sounds will muffle and eventually disappear. The point where the *sounds disappear completely* is the **Diastolic pressure**.
7. Completely deflate the cuff and remove it.

*(Wait at least 2 minutes before running a second check on the same arm.)*

## Part D — Respiratory Volumes (Spirometry)

The lungs do not hold a single, fixed volume of air; they fill and empty dynamically based on need. The **Spiropet** measures exhaled volume mechanically.

1. **Safety check:** Insert a **clean, unused mouthpiece** into the inlet tube. Always hold the Spiropet upright.
2. **Reset the dial:** Ensure the pointing needle is on the zero mark.

**Measurement 1: Tidal Volume (TV)**
Tidal volume is the amount of air inhaled or exhaled during normal, quiet breathing.
1. Take a normal relaxed breath in.
2. Place your lips tightly over the mouthpiece and exhale *normally* (do not force air out).
3. Record the measurement. (It may be very small, around 400-500 mL).

**Measurement 2: Vital Capacity (VC)**
Vital capacity is the absolute maximum amount of air you can exhale after taking the deepest possible breath.
1. Reset the dial to zero.
2. Inhale as deeply as you possibly can.
3. Place your lips tightly over the mouthpiece. Exhale as fully and completely as possible into the device until you literally cannot push any more air out.
4. Record the measurement (typically 3,000–5,000 mL, depending on body size/sex).
