**SUPPORT** — The skeleton gives your body shape and holds soft tissues upright.

Try this: Stand up straight, then deliberately relax all your postural muscles. Notice how you start to sag — that's your skeleton doing the job.

Feel this: Run your fingers along your spine (vertebral column) from the base of your skull down to your tailbone.

---

**PROTECTION** — Bones encase and shield vital organs.

Feel this:

- Gently place both hands on either side of your skull. Tap lightly — the bone is your brain's armor.
- Place one hand flat on your sternum (breastbone). The ribcage beneath protects your heart and lungs.
- Place your hands on your hip bones (iliac crests). The pelvis cradles abdominal organs.

---

**MOVEMENT** — Bones act as levers; muscles pull on them to create motion.

Do this: Flex and extend your elbow while placing your opposite hand on your bicep and tricep. You can feel the muscles contract as they pull the bones.

---

**MINERAL STORAGE** — Bones store about 99% of the body's calcium and a large share of phosphorus.

Think about this: When blood calcium drops too low, osteoclasts dissolve bone matrix to release calcium back into the blood. This is why calcium in your diet matters — your body will steal it from your bones otherwise.

---

**HEMATOPOIESIS** — Red bone marrow produces blood cells.

Think about this: Blood cells are produced in the red marrow found in flat bones (skull, sternum, ribs, pelvic bones) and at the ends of some long bones. In adults, yellow marrow (mostly fat) fills the shaft of long bones.

## Part 2: Skeleton Identification — Axial vs. Appendicular

> **Learning Goal:** Distinguish the axial and appendicular skeleton and identify major bones on the classroom skeleton and on yourself.

### 2A: Background — The Two Divisions

The **axial skeleton** (80 bones) forms the central axis of the body:

- **Skull** — cranium + facial bones
- **Vertebral column** — 7 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 1 sacrum (fused), 1 coccyx (fused)
- **Thoracic cage** — sternum + 12 pairs of ribs

The **appendicular skeleton** (126 bones) consists of the limbs and girdles that attach them:

- **Pectoral girdle** — clavicle + scapula (each side)
- **Upper limb** — humerus, radius, ulna, carpals (8), metacarpals (5), phalanges (14) per side
- **Pelvic girdle** — hip bones (2 fused ilium + ischium + pubis each side) + sacrum
- **Lower limb** — femur, patella, tibia, fibula, tarsals (7), metatarsals (5), phalanges (14) per side

> **Total:** 206 bones in the adult human body (babies are born with ~270–300; many fuse during development)

## Part 3: Long Bone Anatomy — Structure and Function

> **Learning Goal:** Identify the major structural regions of a long bone and explain how structure relates to function.

### 3A: Background — Anatomy of a Long Bone

All long bones share the same fundamental structure:

| Structure | Location | Function |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Diaphysis** | Shaft (long middle portion) | Main weight-bearing region; made of compact bone |
| **Epiphysis** (proximal & distal) | Expanded ends | Forms joints; contains spongy bone with red marrow |
| **Periosteum** | Outer fibrous covering of diaphysis | Contains osteoblasts for growth & repair; attaches tendons/ligaments |
| **Endosteum** | Lines the internal bone surfaces | Contains osteoblasts and osteoclasts for remodeling |
| **Medullary cavity** | Central hollow of diaphysis | Contains yellow marrow (fat) in adults |
| **Articular cartilage** | Covers epiphyses at joint surfaces | Smooth surface for low-friction movement; absorbs shock |
| **Epiphyseal line (plate)** | Between diaphysis and epiphysis | Growth plate in children/teens; ossifies into epiphyseal LINE in adults |

### 3B: Compact Bone vs. Spongy Bone

| Feature | Compact Bone | Spongy Bone |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Location | Outer shell of all bones; shaft of long bones | Inner regions; ends of long bones; flat bones |
| Structural unit | Osteon (Haversian system) — concentric rings of matrix around a central canal | Trabeculae — thin irregular struts arranged along stress lines |
| Weight | Heavy | Lightweight |
| Function | Withstand compression and torsion forces | Reduce weight while maintaining strength; houses red marrow |
| Blood vessels | Run through central (Haversian) and perforating (Volkmann) canals | Spaces between trabeculae carry blood directly |

> **Key insight:** An engineer designing a scaffold to be strong but light would end up with a structure very similar to spongy bone — thin struts aligned along the directions of greatest stress.

## Part 4: Bone Cells and Remodeling

> **Learning Goal:** Understand how three cell types coordinate bone maintenance and remodeling throughout life.

### 4A: The Three Players

| Cell Type | Origin | Location | What It Does |
|-----------|--------|----------|--------------|
| **Osteoblast** | Mesenchymal stem cells | On bone surfaces | **Builds** bone — secretes collagen matrix (osteoid), then mineralizes it |
| **Osteocyte** | Former osteoblasts trapped in matrix | Inside lacunae (tiny spaces in matrix) | **Maintains** bone — monitors mechanical strain, coordinates remodeling signals |
| **Osteoclast** | Monocyte/macrophage lineage | On bone surfaces | **Breaks down** bone — secretes acid and enzymes to dissolve matrix; releases stored calcium |

> **Memory tip:** Blast = Build, Clast = Cut (think "iconoclast" = one who breaks things)

### 4B: The Remodeling Cycle

Bone is not static — it is continuously remodeled throughout life in a coordinated sequence:

1. **Activation** — Osteocytes detect micro-cracks or hormonal signals (e.g., low blood calcium, mechanical unloading)
2. **Resorption** — Osteoclasts dissolve old bone, releasing calcium into bloodstream
3. **Reversal** — Osteoclasts leave; osteoblasts are recruited
4. **Formation** — Osteoblasts deposit new bone matrix and mineralize it
5. **Quiescence** — New bone rests until next remodeling cycle

> **Clinical connection:** Bisphosphonate drugs (e.g., Fosamax, used in osteoporosis) work by **inhibiting osteoclasts**, slowing resorption so bone is retained.

> **Learning Goal:** Classify joints by movement type and demonstrate range of motion on your own body.

### 5A: Joint Classification Overview

Joints are classified two ways — by **structure** (what holds them together) and by **movement** (how much they move):

**By Movement (Functional Classification):**

| Class | Movement | Examples |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Synarthrosis** | Immovable | Skull sutures, tooth-to-socket (gomphosis) |
| **Amphiarthrosis** | Slightly movable | Pubic symphysis, intervertebral discs, joints between ribs and sternum |
| **Diarthrosis** (synovial) | Freely movable | Shoulder, knee, elbow, hip, knuckles |

**Synovial Joint Subtypes (by shape and movement):**

| Type | Movement Allowed | Body Example | Movement on Skeleton |
|------|-----------------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Hinge** | Flexion / Extension (1 plane) | Elbow, knee, ankle, interphalangeal joints | Bend and straighten |
| **Ball-and-socket** | All directions + rotation | Hip, shoulder | Swing arm in full circle |
| **Pivot** | Rotation only | Atlantoaxial joint (C1–C2), proximal radioulnar | Shake head "no"; pronate/supinate forearm |
| **Condyloid** (ellipsoidal) | Flexion/extension + abduction/adduction, no rotation | Wrist (radiocarpal), knuckles (metacarpophalangeal) | Wrist circles (circumduction) |
| **Saddle** | Like condyloid + greater range | First carpometacarpal (thumb base) | Opposable thumb movements |
| **Plane** (gliding) | Sliding in one or two planes | Intercarpal, intertarsal, facet joints of vertebrae | Small gliding motions |

## Part 6: Human vs. Mammal Skeleton Comparison

> **Learning Goal:** Compare the human skeleton to a mammal skeleton and connect structural differences to function and evolutionary history.

### 6A: Background — Homologous Structures

Mammals share a common vertebrate ancestor, so their bones are **homologous** — made of the same bones, modified for different functions. The same five elements (one upper bone, two lower bones, several small bones, and digits) appear in the human arm, horse leg, dolphin flipper, and bat wing.

> **Homologous structures** = same evolutionary origin, different function
> **Analogous structures** = different origin, same function (e.g., bird wing vs. butterfly wing)

## Part 7: Skeletal Health and Disorders

> **Learning Goal:** Connect skeletal disorders to underlying biology and identify prevention/treatment strategies.

### 7A: Osteoporosis — Silent Bone Loss

**Background:**
Osteoporosis occurs when bone resorption (osteoclast activity) outpaces bone deposition (osteoblast activity), leading to net loss of bone mass. Bone becomes porous, brittle, and highly fracture-prone.

**Risk factors include:**

- Aging (especially post-menopausal women — estrogen normally restrains osteoclasts)
- Low calcium and Vitamin D intake
- Physical inactivity / sedentary lifestyle
- Smoking, heavy alcohol use
- Family history
- Small body frame (less bone mass reserve)

**Prevention strategies:**

- Adequate dietary calcium (1000–1200 mg/day for adults)
- Vitamin D (sunlight + diet/supplements)
- Weight-bearing exercise (walking, weightlifting) — stimulates osteoblasts via mechanical stress
- Avoid smoking and excessive alcohol

### 7B: Fracture Healing — The Four Stages

When a bone breaks, healing occurs in four overlapping stages:

| Stage | Timeline | What Happens |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **1. Hematoma Formation** | Hours to days | Blood vessels rupture; clot forms; dead bone cells release signals |
| **2. Fibrocartilaginous Callus** | Days to 3 weeks | Fibroblasts and chondroblasts invade clot; lay down fibrocartilage "soft callus" |
| **3. Bony Callus** | 3–6 weeks | Osteoblasts replace fibrocartilage with spongy bone; "hard callus" bridges the gap |
| **4. Bone Remodeling** | Months to years | Osteoclasts and osteoblasts reshape and compact the repair; bone gradually returns to original architecture |

### 7C: Arthritis — Joint Disorders

| | **Osteoarthritis (OA)** | **Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Cause** | Mechanical wear-and-tear of articular cartilage | Autoimmune — immune system attacks synovial membrane |
| **Who** | Usually older adults; affects weight-bearing joints | Any age; often young-to-middle-aged adults |

| **Morning stiffness** | Brief (< 30 min) | Prolonged (> 1 hour) |
| **Systemic effects** | Localized | Yes — fatigue, fever, organ involvement possible |

### Bone Cell Function Summary

| Cell | Function | Analogy |
|------|----------|---------|
| Osteoblast | Deposits bone matrix | Building crew |
| Osteocyte | Maintains and monitors | Site foreman |

### Joint Mobility Mnemonic

**S-A-D** (from least to most movement):

- **S**ynarthrosis → stuck (immovable)
- **A**mphiarthrosis → a little movement
- **D**iarthrosis → dance freely (most movement)

### Bone Structure at a Glance

| Bone Type | Structure | Strength |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| Compact | Osteons (Haversian systems) | Resists torsion, compression |
| Spongy | Trabeculae along stress lines | Strong per unit weight; houses marrow |
