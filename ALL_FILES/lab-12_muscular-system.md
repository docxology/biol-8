## Background: Three Types of Muscle Tissue

| Feature | Skeletal Muscle | Cardiac Muscle | Smooth Muscle |
|---------|----------------|----------------|---------------|
| **Location** | Attached to bones | Heart wall | Walls of hollow organs (stomach, blood vessels, bladder) |
| **Appearance** | Striated (striped) | Striated | Non-striated |
| **Control** | Voluntary | Involuntary | Involuntary |
| **Cell shape** | Long, cylindrical, multinucleate | Branched, intercalated discs | Spindle-shaped, uninucleate |
| **Speed** | Fast to slow (fiber type) | Rhythmic, sustained | Slow, sustained |
| **Example action** | Picking up a cup | Pumping blood | Moving food through intestines |

## Background: How Skeletal Muscle Contracts

Skeletal muscles are organized from large to small:
**Muscle → Fascicle → Muscle fiber (cell) → Myofibril → Sarcomere**

The **sarcomere** is the functional unit. It contains:
- **Thick filaments** — myosin (has "heads" that form cross-bridges)
- **Thin filaments** — actin (plus regulatory proteins troponin and tropomyosin)

**Sliding Filament Theory:** When calcium is released from the sarcoplasmic reticulum, it binds troponin, which moves tropomyosin off of actin binding sites. Myosin heads attach, pull actin filaments inward, and release (using ATP). The sarcomere shortens — the muscle contracts.

**Neuromuscular Junction:** A motor neuron signals the muscle fiber by releasing **acetylcholine (ACh)**, which triggers an action potential in the muscle fiber, leading to calcium release and contraction.

**Muscle Fiber Types:**

| | Slow-Twitch (Type I) | Fast-Twitch (Type II) |
|---|---|---|
| **Speed** | Slower | Faster |
| **Fatigue resistance** | High | Low |
| **Energy source** | Aerobic (oxygen) | Anaerobic (glycogen) |
| **Best for** | Endurance (walking, posture) | Power/speed (sprinting, jumping) |
| **Myoglobin content** | High (red) | Lower (white) |

## Experiment 2: Ruler-Drop Reaction Time

### Background

Reaction time is the time between a stimulus (seeing the ruler drop) and a voluntary motor response (catching it). The signal travels from your **eye → visual cortex → motor cortex → motor neuron → neuromuscular junction → muscle fiber**. This pathway takes measurable time — typically 150–250 ms in healthy adults. You can convert the distance a ruler falls (in cm) to reaction time using physics:

> **Distance fallen under gravity:**  d = ½ g t²  →  **t = √(2d / g)**
>
> Where g ≈ 980 cm/s²  →  simplified: **t (seconds) ≈ √(d_cm / 490)**

| Distance Caught (cm) | Reaction Time (ms) |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| 5 | 101 |
| 10 | 143 |
| 15 | 175 |
| 20 | 202 |
| 25 | 226 |
| 30 | 247 |

### Procedure

1. **Subject** sits with dominant forearm resting on the table, hand extended over the edge, thumb and forefinger open and ready to catch — **do not pre-tension your hand**.
2. **Partner** holds the ruler/meter stick vertically so the **0 cm mark** is level with the subject's thumb and forefinger.
3. **Partner** drops the ruler without warning. **Subject** catches it as fast as possible.
4. Record the **cm mark at the top of the subject's thumb** when caught. This is your distance dropped.
5. Perform **10 trials**. Record each distance.
6. Use the conversion table above (or formula) to convert each distance to reaction time in milliseconds.
7. Switch roles so both partners are tested.
