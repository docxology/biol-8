## Background: How Skeletal Muscle Contracts

### Structural Organization

Skeletal muscles are organized from large to small:

> **Muscle → Fascicle → Muscle fiber (cell) → Myofibril → Sarcomere**

Each **muscle fiber** is a single, long, multinucleate cell packed with **myofibrils** — bundles of contractile proteins organized into repeating units called **sarcomeres**.

### The Sarcomere and Sliding Filament Theory

The **sarcomere** is the functional unit of contraction. It contains two interleaved sets of protein filaments:
- **Thick filaments** — myosin, with projecting "heads" that form **cross-bridges** with actin
- **Thin filaments** — actin, coated by the regulatory proteins **troponin** and **tropomyosin**

**How a contraction happens (step by step):**
1. A nerve signal arrives → ACh is released at the neuromuscular junction
2. ACh binds receptors on the muscle fiber → action potential travels along the sarcolemma and into T-tubules
3. T-tubules signal the **sarcoplasmic reticulum** to release Ca²⁺ into the cytoplasm
4. Ca²⁺ binds **troponin**, which shifts **tropomyosin** away from myosin-binding sites on actin
5. Myosin heads attach to actin and perform the **power stroke** — pulling thin filaments toward the center of the sarcomere
6. ATP binds myosin → head detaches → re-cocks for another stroke
7. Sarcomeres shorten across the whole fiber → muscle shortens → force is generated

### Neuromuscular Junction and Motor Units

A **neuromuscular junction (NMJ)** is the synapse between a motor neuron and a muscle fiber. The neurotransmitter **acetylcholine (ACh)** diffuses across the synaptic cleft and triggers the muscle action potential. After the signal, the enzyme **acetylcholinesterase** rapidly breaks down ACh so the muscle can relax.

A **motor unit** = one motor neuron + all the muscle fibers it controls. Recruiting more motor units produces greater force. Small motor units (few fibers) allow fine control (eye muscles); large motor units (many fibers) generate powerful contractions (quadriceps).

### Contraction Types

- **Isotonic contraction** — muscle changes length while tension is relatively constant; movement occurs *(e.g., picking up a glass, doing a bicep curl)*
- **Isometric contraction** — muscle generates tension but does **not** shorten; no movement *(e.g., holding a plank, carrying groceries without moving your arm)*

### Muscle Fiber Types and Energy Systems

| | Slow-Twitch (Type I) | Fast-Twitch (Type IIa) | Fast-Twitch (Type IIx) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Speed** | Slow | Moderate-fast | Very fast |
| **Fatigue resistance** | Very high | Moderate | Low |
| **Primary energy source** | Aerobic (oxidative) | Mixed aerobic/anaerobic | Anaerobic (glycolytic) |
| **Best for** | Endurance, posture | Sustained power | Short explosive bursts |
| **Myoglobin / color** | High (red) | Moderate | Low (white) |
| **Example use** | Marathon running | 400 m swim | 100 m sprint |

**Key energy concepts:**
- **ATP** is the immediate energy currency; muscles store only ~2–3 seconds worth
- **Creatine phosphate** replenishes ATP rapidly for ~10 seconds of intense activity
- **Anaerobic glycolysis** generates ATP quickly but produces **lactate** (and H⁺), contributing to acidification and fatigue
- **Aerobic respiration** (using oxygen and mitochondria) is slower but far more efficient — it dominates during sustained activity in slow-twitch fibers

## Experiment 1: Grip Fatigue Test

### Background

When you repeatedly squeeze a muscle, it eventually fatigues — it produces less force or cannot sustain the same rate of contractions. Fatigue in fast-twitch fibers happens quickly (ATP and glycogen deplete, lactic acid builds). Slow-twitch fibers are more fatigue-resistant.
