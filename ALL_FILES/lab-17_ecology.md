## Purpose

**Bioaccumulation and biomagnification of DDT:** You will observe how a contaminant accumulates in organisms and becomes more concentrated at higher levels of a simple food web, using beads to model producers, fish, and a fish-eating bird.

---

## Learning objectives

- Explain **bioaccumulation** (buildup of a contaminant within an individual) and **biomagnification** (increasing concentration at higher trophic levels).
- Track **DDT bead** counts through a model food chain: producers → stickleback → cutthroat trout → osprey.
- Interpret bead data to describe how **DDT** moves and concentrates in a simplified aquatic web.
- Connect **DDT** to documented effects on **top predators** (e.g., eggshell thinning in birds) and to **indirect** effects on other populations when a predator declines.

## Materials

- **Shaker cup (plastic)** — holds the producer population
- **9 small cups** — stickleback fish
- **3 medium cups** — cutthroat trout
- **1 large cup** — osprey
- **20 beads of one color** — producer biomass
- **10 beads of a second color** — DDT (pesticide)
- **Scratch paper** (optional) — tally DDT in each small/medium/large cup before you copy **summary** numbers into the data table

**Total:** 30 beads in the shaker at the start, with **DDT making up 1/3** of the starting mixture (10 of 30 beads).

---

## Background

This lab explores **bioaccumulation** (buildup of contaminants within an individual organism) and **biomagnification** (increasing concentration at higher trophic levels), using **DDT** as the case study. DDT was inexpensive to manufacture and persistent in the environment. It enters aquatic systems by binding to plankton, accumulates in their cells, passes to fish that eat the plankton, and then to birds that eat the fish. In birds, DDT disrupts **eggshell formation**, producing brittle shells that break before hatching.

## Procedure

### Setup

1. Place all **30 beads** (20 producer + 10 DDT) into the **shaker cup**.
2. In the data table, record the **DDT-to-total ratio** in the shaker (**10/30 = 1/3**). Shake thoroughly to mix.

### Trophic level 1 → 2 (producers → stickleback)

3. Remove **3 beads** from the shaker and place them into **each** of the **9** stickleback cups (27 beads moved).
4. Tally the **number of DDT beads in each of the 9** cups on **scratch paper** (or the margin) if you like. You do **not** need to list every cup on this handout. For **this trial**, compute the **average DDT per stickleback** = **(sum of DDT in all 9 cups) ÷ 9** and enter that **one number** in the **Data** table in the **column** for the trial you just ran (**Trial 1, 2, or 3**; see **Data** below).

### Trophic level 2 → 3 (stickleback → cutthroat trout)

5. Each trout eats **2 stickleback** — empty **2 small cups** into **each** of the **3** medium (trout) cups.
6. Tally **DDT in each of the 3** trout cups if needed, then for **this trial** record **average DDT per trout** = **(sum of DDT in the 3 trout cups) ÷ 3** in the **Data** table in the same trial **column** as in step 4.

### Trophic level 3 → 4 (trout → osprey)

7. Each osprey eats **2 trout** — empty **2 medium cups** into the **large** (osprey) cup.
8. For **this trial**, record **total DDT in the osprey** (one **large** cup) in the **Data** table in the same trial **column**. (There is one osprey: “average DDT per osprey” is the **same** as that total.)

### Trials and calculations

9. **Repeat** the full procedure (setup through osprey) for **Trials 2 and 3**. Each new trial, **replace** all 30 beads in the shaker, remix, and fill in the same rows for that trial’s column.  
10. For each row, you may also compute a **mean across the three trials** in the last column. Use the **Data** table as your only required record on this page.
